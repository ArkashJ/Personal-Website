## What actually shipped

W19 starts with three long-form watches that all point at the same thing —
**the substrate is being formalised**. Kalshi formalised event hedging into a
regulated exchange. Snap is formalising "compute as glasses + private
messaging" instead of "compute as phone + endless feed". Stripe formalised
agentic commerce into two protocols (MPP + UCP) and a real-time Postgres view
of your business. Different domains, same move: turn an emerging behaviour
into a standard before the messy version locks in.

Foundry v0.5 plumbing continues. Cattle Logic ranch learnings from W18 are
being written up.

## Uncapped — Tarek Mansour on Building Kalshi

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://youtu.be/Tt6ozrcjLyQ)** · Jack Altman with Tarek Mansour, co-founder/CEO of Kalshi

### Background and Genesis (0:29 – 3:55)

Born in California, raised in Lebanon. The volatility of his environment turned math into a stability instrument early. MIT → Goldman Sachs → Citadel, where he noticed something specific about how traders actually worked: **most ideas were opinions about future events** (Brexit outcomes, 2016 election, Fed decisions) being expressed through indirect proxies — currency pairs, vol structures, sector ETFs. Traditional markets weren't built to clear opinions on outcomes; they were built to clear claims on assets. The mismatch produced "backwards" trading and missed risk transfer.

### YC Hackathon → Y Combinator (6:42 – 8:02)

October 2018. He and his co-founder built an **order book for event contracts** at the YC hackathon. Judges told them flatly: _not allowed in the US_. They won the hackathon anyway. Entering YC was hard — no product, no customers, just a regulatory mountain.

### Regulatory Battle (11:06 – 18:32)

- **Nov 2020** — granted regulated exchange approval (CFTC).
- **2023** — election market blocked; multiple delays.
- After exhausting the cooperative path, they **sued the government**.
- **Oct 2024** — landmark legal win redefining the line between gambling and financial markets, establishing Kalshi as a regulated exchange and clearing house.

The legal milestone matters not just for Kalshi but for the category: it created a federal precedent that event contracts on real-world outcomes can be financial instruments rather than gaming products.

### Market Structure (18:36 – 18:50)

Kalshi is **not a casino**. It's an open book where users trade against each other; Kalshi takes a small transaction fee. There is no house taking the other side. That is the structural difference — and it's what allows institutions to participate with a straight face.

### Hedging Real Life (35:51 – 38:00)

The pitch isn't "predict the future for fun." It's **hedge the things that actually move your life and balance sheet**: CPI prints, FOMC decisions, weather events for energy and ag exposure, election outcomes for regulated industries. The fairness argument: you can't beat hedge funds at options pricing, but you can buy a fixed-payoff contract on the underlying event at the same price they can.

### Team Structure (41:47 – 43:30)

**~127 people. No traditional managerial layers.** The org is self-organizing — the team's job is to identify the company's most critical problem and route themselves toward it. Tarek's framing: high-output autonomy beats hierarchy when the problem set is fast-moving and the bar for individual contributors is high. He also names what most founders don't say out loud: **firing is hard**, and the only way to keep the bar high is to keep doing it.

### What I'm taking away

The thing that's underrated about Kalshi is the **legitimacy compounding**. Each regulatory win made the next harder to deny. The 2024 case wasn't a leap — it was the inevitable consequence of three years of patient ratcheting. Building inside hostile regulation rewards founders who can take a punch repeatedly.

## Cheeky Pint — Evan Spiegel on Spectacles, Snap, and Computing Made Human

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://youtu.be/8fZNK-M-77I)** · Snap CEO Evan Spiegel

### Spectacles and the Future of Computing

Spiegel's framing for Spectacles: **"making computing human again"**. The phone isolates — head down, screen between you and the world. See-through glasses are social by default. He explicitly contrasts with full VR headsets (Vision Pro): they're powerful but anti-social. Spectacles are the bet that a real spatial computer at **glasses weight** will win because it doesn't ask you to leave the room you're in.

### The Stack

To hit the form factor and the responsiveness, Snap rebuilt the OS. **Snap OS is Linux-based, proprietary** — they explicitly avoided Android because the bloat costs them latency, battery, and weight. The decision to build the OS top-to-bottom is the same instinct Apple acted on with Vision Pro: at glasses weight, you cannot afford a generic mobile stack.

### AI in Production

**Over two-thirds of new code at Snap is AI-written.** This is the line that should make every CTO ask whether their org is still pretending this is a year-out conversation. Spiegel doesn't dwell — he treats it as the new baseline.

### Snap's Core Philosophy (28:13 – 34:08)

Snapchat distinguishes itself by **separating social (private messaging with close friends) from media (Discover)**. The endless public feed is the failure mode of social media. Spiegel credits this separation for Snap's relatively positive impact on user wellbeing. The financial argument tracks too: avoiding feed-driven engagement loops removed the perverse incentive that drives most platforms toward outrage.

### Business Mix

Two revenue streams: advertising + **Snapchat+ subscription at 25M subscribers**. The subscription is real revenue, not a vanity number — it changes the margin profile and the incentive structure when a meaningful chunk of users pay you directly.

### Teens, Policy, and Wellbeing (38:55 – 45:30)

Spiegel pushes back on blanket teen-platform bans: **every child is different**, and policy that treats teens as a uniform mass produces worse outcomes than policy that empowers parents. He cites research suggesting Snapchat usage is associated with **better** relationship outcomes than other platforms, attributable to the friend-graph and messaging-first design.

### Leadership Evolution (59:49 – 1:00:59)

He started as a product builder. The role now is **empathy and inspiration** — getting a large team to attack massive, industry-shaping problems with conviction. Less hands on a Figma file, more hands on the team's morale and clarity of mission.

### Fun Facts

- **Norway** was the first country to embrace Snapchat — high-end device penetration + reliable internet meant the product worked the way it was designed to.
- The interview ended with a darts game in the pub. On brand.

## Stripe Sessions 2026 — Keynote: Projects, Database, MPP, UCP, Treasury, and the Agent Substrate

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://www.youtube.com/live/e13-s0p1tfE)** · Stripe Sessions 2026 Keynote

The Sessions 2026 keynote is the cleanest read so far on **what an agent-native commerce stack looks like**. Stripe is positioning itself as **the economic infrastructure for the AI era** (2:50 – 8:36) and shipping the surface area to back the claim.

### Product Launches

**Stripe Projects (11:02 – 14:17).** Programmatic deployment of applications. Agents can spin up services, databases (Supabase), and hosting (Vercel) from inside a Stripe-managed control plane. The point isn't "Stripe deploys your app" — it's that the deployment graph is now legible to a payment system, so spend, metering, and reconciliation flow through one substrate.

**Agentic Commerce Suite (19:40 – 22:27).** Makes products **legible to agents** — structured product feeds, machine-readable pricing, deterministic checkout flows. Includes support for the **Model Payment Protocol (MPP)** for agent-to-agent transactions.

**Checkout Studio (24:01 – 26:31).** Visual builder + analytics dashboard for high-performing checkout flows. Custom embedded forms, optimization signals.

**Stripe Database (57:48 – 58:10).** **Postgres-based**. Real-time queryable view of your Stripe business data — for developers and their agents. This is the move that matters most to me as a builder: agents reason in SQL-like patterns; giving them a Postgres surface over your business is the cleanest substrate possible.

**Metronome + Tempo (45:47 – 50:55).** Real-time metering, rating, and **payment streaming**. New business models become possible: charge as tokens are consumed, not at the end of a billing period. Metering becomes a primitive, not a billing-system afterthought.

**Stripe Signals (1:27:28 – 1:28:09).** Intelligence API — real-time risk scores and insights on customers and transactions, callable from any system.

**Stripe Treasury (1:40:15 – 1:48:21).** Financial operating system: multicurrency accounts, FDIC-insured balances in the US, payouts across **160 countries**, and **stablecoin rail support** — enabling cross-border money movement that doesn't go through correspondent banking.

### Strategic Updates

**Platform Growth Studio (1:54:31 – 1:56:51).** Cross-platform recommendation engine surfacing growth opportunities from aggregate Stripe data.

**Public Roadmap (2:08:33 – 2:09:12).** Stripe published a comprehensive **public roadmap at stripe.com/roadmap** through Q1 2027. This is unusual for an infrastructure company at this scale — and an explicit signal that Stripe wants downstream partners betting on specific dates.

### The Two Protocols

The structural news of the keynote: Stripe shipped two protocols.

#### Model Payment Protocol — MPP (14:59 – 18:20)

**Purpose.** An open standard for agents to **purchase goods and services autonomously**. The hard problem MPP solves: pricing pages, forms, and payment flows were designed for humans, and agents fail at them in ways that are non-obvious until you watch one try.

**Mechanism.** A communication layer that lets a service signal to an agent that **payment is required**, then exchanges value directly between two autonomous endpoints. Integrates with the **Link wallet** so a human can review and authorize agent-initiated payments via biometric thumbprint. The control surface is "human approves a budget envelope, agent transacts inside it."

#### Universal Commerce Protocol — UCP (2:03:01 – 2:04:16)

**Purpose.** A unified commerce standard across platforms — eliminates fragmentation across the buy / fulfill / refund / dispute lifecycle.

**Context.** Pioneered by **Stripe and Google**. As of this keynote, **Meta, Amazon, Salesforce, and Microsoft** have joined the UCP general committee. The goal: one standard to build on, one specification to implement, one community to support — so agentic commerce doesn't fragment into platform-specific dialects the way bot APIs did.

### Why this keynote matters

Two patterns landed in the same keynote:

1. **Make business state legible to agents** — Stripe Database (Postgres surface), Stripe Signals (intelligence API), Checkout Studio analytics.
2. **Standardize the agent-to-agent commerce path** — MPP for payments, UCP for the broader commerce journey.

The economics implied: as soon as agents can read business state and transact across a standard protocol, the unit cost of a transaction collapses and metering pressure rises. Tempo + Metronome are the answer to the metering pressure. It's a coherent stack, not a list of features.
