## Stripe Sessions — AI Replatforming Global Trade

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-vRY2dtD7iQ)** · John Collison & Emily Sans

> **TL;DR.** AI is replatforming global trade — solopreneurs scaling to real revenue, startups born globally on day one, and agents transacting with stablecoins via the Tempo CLI.

- **Solopreneurs at scale.** ~5M Americans earn their living solo; the >$100k cohort is breaking historical numbers.
- **Globally-native by default.** Stripe AI startups already earn most revenue internationally in year one.
- **Agentic commerce.** Levels 1→5 of autonomy; machine-to-machine micro-payments with stablecoins bypass fiat rails.
- **Complement rule.** As intelligence gets cheap, complements (proprietary data, network effects, physical-world integration) get expensive.
- **Solow Paradox.** Productivity dividend lags structural workflow replatforming — 1882 electrification → 1920s output curve.

### Economic Dynamism and Business Formation

**Solopreneurs scaling.** There has been a significant surge in non-employer firm formations. AI lets solopreneurs achieve real scale — nearly 5 million Americans now earn their living this way, with a growing cohort crossing $100k in revenue. The class of 2026 is beating historical numbers.

**Globally-native startups.** Modern startups launch in multiple markets from day one. AI startups on Stripe often see the median firm earning most of its revenue internationally, selling into dozens of countries within their first year of operation. The old playbook of "go domestic first, expand later" is dead.

### Agentic Commerce and Infrastructure

**Levels of autonomy.** Collison defines the progression from Level 1 (software filling forms / in-app checkout) to Level 3–5 (agents making fully independent purchasing decisions without human approval in the loop).

**Machine-to-machine payments.** The critical technical shift: autonomous payments. The keynote demos an agent using a stablecoin wallet via the **Tempo CLI** to execute micro-purchases for data — bypassing the latency and cost of fiat-based payment rails entirely. This is what "agentic commerce" looks like at the infrastructure layer: programmable money that settles in milliseconds with no interchange fee drag.

**Orchestration.** Tools like Stripe Projects and API-based integrations now handle deployment, API key management, and procurement autonomously. The grunt work is being automated away.

### Value Complements in the Age of AI

**The complement rule.** When intelligence becomes abundant and cheap, its complements increase in value. Key complements Collison identifies:

- **Proprietary data** — Data becomes a high-value training and reasoning asset. Companies are increasingly walling off data from AI crawlers to monetize it directly rather than giving it away.
- **Network effects** — As AI simplifies discovery, marketplaces that aggregate buyers and sellers command higher take rates. The aggregator advantage compounds.
- **Real-world integration** — John Deere is the example: enduring moats come from combining AI software with physical, hardware-heavy operations (GPS arrays, sensor networks, global physical assets). Software-only moats erode fast; physical-plus-software moats don't.

### Productivity and the Solow Paradox

Drawing on the history of electrification (first commercial plant 1882, productivity dividend arrived in the 1920s) and early computing, Collison and Sans address the **Solow Paradox** — the phenomenon where technological breakthroughs show up everywhere except in official productivity statistics. Their argument: the lag exists because the economy must undergo structural _replatforming_ and workflow redesign before the productivity dividends appear in measured output. We are still in the replatforming phase. The numbers will come.

## Odd Lots — KMT, China, Taiwan, and Why a Land Invasion Can't Win Yet

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lxmt7rz4DLQ)** · Guest: Eyck Freymann

> **TL;DR.** Taiwan is a regime-legitimacy story for Xi, not a chip story. China has built sanctions shock absorbers by studying Russia. The right strategy is avalanche decoupling, not total decoupling.

- **Silicon shield.** A Taiwan disruption is a hard reset of the post-1989 economic order — TSMC is the consequence, not the cause.
- **Xi's motivation.** Reunification is about CCP legitimacy and the unfinished civil war; chips are downstream.
- **Ruble playbook.** China stockpiled oil/cotton/chips and watched Russia survive — capital controls + 20% rates + state ruble payments.
- **Avalanche decoupling.** Strategic, sector-specific de-risking _before_ a crisis beats trying to fully decouple during one.
- **DPP vs KMT.** DPP = distinct Taiwanese identity, US-aligned. KMT = one-China framing, cautious engagement with Beijing.

### The Silicon Shield and Economic Order

A blockade or conflict involving Taiwan isn't a regional security story — it's a catastrophic threat to the global economy. TSMC and high-end semiconductors for AI make a shutdown a "hard reset" of the post-1989 global economic order. The chips are a _consequence_ of why Taiwan matters geopolitically, not the primary cause of the conflict risk.

### Xi's Real Motivation

China's interest in Taiwan is primarily about political legitimacy and the unfinished business of the Chinese Civil War, not chip strategy. The party views national "rejuvenation" as incomplete without sovereignty over the island. Xi has staked his personal legacy on this — it predates and supersedes the semiconductor story.

### Economic Shock Absorbers and the Ruble Trade

China has built significant stockpiles — oil, cotton, semiconductors — designed to outlast a Russia-style sanctions shock. The ruble playbook: Russia imposed capital controls, pushed interest rates to 20%, state enterprises paid in rubles, imports collapsed, exports surged on high oil prices, and the ruble recovered. China has studied every step.

Freymann's prescription: **"avalanche decoupling"** — gradual, strategic de-risking in specific critical sectors rather than attempting an impossible total decoupling overnight. The goal is to remove leverage before a crisis, not scramble during one.

### Military Dynamics

China has numerically superior forces, but the US may hold a structural edge in naval warfare specifically. Unlike land attrition (where mass matters), sea warfare involves specialized platforms where early losses can cascade into structural collapse of capability. The Strait of Hormuz lesson: military capability alone is insufficient if political and economic systems can't sustain long-term disruption. Deterrence requires bipartisan consensus on economic resilience and tighter allied coordination.

### Taiwan's Domestic Politics

- **DPP** (Democratic Progressive Party) — emphasizes a distinct Taiwanese identity, strong US alignment
- **KMT** (Kuomintang) — maintains traditional one-China framing, favors cautious diplomatic engagement with Beijing

The domestic split matters enormously for how Taiwan would respond under pressure. KMT alignment with the "one China" principle creates real tension with DPP governance when cross-strait relations deteriorate.

## Odd Lots — METR: Measuring AI Autonomy with Time Horizon Charts

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sVfWn4PQ7vM)** · Guests: Joel Becker & Chris Painter (METR)

> **TL;DR.** "Time horizon" measures the human-hours of a task an AI can solve at 50% — capability is doubling every ~4 months, but benchmarks miss the messy real-world tax.

- **The metric.** Time horizon = task difficulty (skilled-human hours), measured at 50% success rate — not how long a model can run.
- **Why 50%?** Statistically stable. 99%-thresholds are sensitive to label noise and need huge samples; 80% curves are far less dramatic.
- **Doubling.** Capability is doubling roughly every 4 months; frontier-lab compute spend tracks the trend.
- **Misreading.** The most common error: assuming the chart shows duration of autonomous operation. It doesn't — it shows task difficulty.
- **Reality gap.** Real codebases, collaboration, verification overhead aren't captured. Capability ceiling ≠ deployment reality.

### The Core Metric

METR defines **"time horizon"** as the length of a task — measured by how long a skilled human takes to complete it — that an AI model can solve at a **50% success rate**. They focus on complex autonomous engineering and ML research tasks to track the point at which AI could engage in recursive self-improvement or automate its own R&D process.

### Why 50%?

Estimating high-reliability thresholds (e.g., 99%) requires vastly larger sample sizes and is highly sensitive to label noise. 50% is statistically stable and produces the clearest trend line. When you look at the 80% reliability curve instead, the progress appears less dramatic — which is the correct signal that many investment narratives are ignoring.

### The Exponential

Capabilities have been doubling roughly every **4 months**. Frontier lab compute R&D spending has been tracking this growth — the trend is heavily capital-supported. Given the infrastructure already committed for the coming years, significant deceleration is unlikely even if the industry slows at the model layer.

### Common Misreading of the Charts

Most viewers assume the charts show a model working autonomously for a set duration (e.g., "can work for 12 hours"). **Wrong.** They measure task _difficulty_ — how long it takes a skilled human to complete the equivalent work — not elapsed wall-clock time. Joel Becker specifically flags this as the most widespread misinterpretation.

### Real-World Gaps

Benchmark success doesn't map cleanly to productivity. Real tasks involve large messy codebases, collaboration, human verification in non-deterministic workflows. The charts don't capture that friction — they measure capability ceiling, not deployment reality.

### The Baptist and Bootlegger Dynamic

The same companies building cutting-edge models are the most vocal about existential risks. METR operates as a nonprofit specifically to maintain evaluator neutrality — though they depend on access to proprietary models from those same labs. Public awareness of capability trends is a prerequisite for effective governance, even if it distorts investor behavior as a side effect.

## Odd Lots — Dinosaurs as an Alternative Asset Class

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hf4nv3ggdqE)** · Guest: Salomon Aaron (David Aaron Gallery)

> **TL;DR.** Stan T-Rex ($31M) and Apex Stegosaurus ($45M) reset fossil pricing into the tens of millions. Authentication is dominated by bone maps, and sophisticated buyers now pre-fund excavation.

- **Market reset.** Pricing moved from low-millions to tens of millions; comps are thin and recent.
- **Due diligence.** US private-land specimens only, with GPS, deeds, and in-situ photo/video documentation.
- **Bone maps.** Quantify % real bone vs resin/3D-printed reconstruction — primary fraud vector is composite-as-original.
- **Pre-buys.** Sovereign wealth competition pushes collectors to fund digs before pieces are uncovered.
- **Public/private tension.** Private capital makes large-scale exploration economic; museum loans (e.g., Colchester) are a workable model.

### Market Structure

High-value fossil discovery is labor-intensive, seasonal, and geologically constrained. The pricing paradigm shifted dramatically: the **Stan T-Rex ($31M)** and **Apex Stegosaurus ($45M)** sales reset expectations from historical low-millions to tens-of-millions. The market still lacks the historical pricing data that anchors traditional art valuation — comps are thin and recent.

### Due Diligence

Because fossil legal status varies by country, the gallery focuses exclusively on American specimens found on private land where title is clear. Minimum documentation required:

- GPS coordinates of the discovery site
- Legal land deeds proving private ownership
- Comprehensive photographic and video documentation of the piece _in situ_ — proving it wasn't assembled from disparate sources after the fact

### Bone Maps

The critical technical challenge: distinguishing real bone mass from resin or 3D-printed reconstruction. Professional paleontologists and conservators produce precise **"bone maps"** quantifying the percentage of original material in each specimen. Misrepresenting a composite specimen as majority-original is the primary fraud vector in this market. This is the equivalent of provenance authentication in the art market, but harder to fake because the science is deterministic.

### The Pre-Buy Strategy

Because sovereign wealth funds and institutional buyers compete intensely for top specimens, sophisticated collectors now fund excavation and preparation _before_ the piece is fully uncovered or assembled. You're betting on what's in the ground before you know how complete it is — high-risk, but the only way to access the best specimens before they go to auction.

### Private Capital and Public Science

A key tension: private ownership vs. public scientific access. Aaron argues that the influx of private capital makes large-scale exploration economically feasible, which can benefit science when specimens are loaned or donated to museums. The Colchester Museum arrangement is cited as a model. Without private money, many of the most significant discoveries would stay in the ground indefinitely.

### Investment Risks

High maintenance costs, regulatory risk (legal status could shift), low liquidity vs. traditional asset classes, and no historical return data. Aaron's position: primary driver should be historical interest, not financial speculation. It's a collectible first, an investment second.

## Bill Ackman — Pershing Square PSUS: Closed-End Fund IPO

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VqG5V6GXC20)** · Bill Ackman, CEO of Pershing Square

> **TL;DR.** PSUS is a closed-end-fund-as-permanent-capital play modeled on Berkshire's insurance float — tax-efficient, no incentive fees, and built to compound NAV without redemption risk.

- **Closed-end structure.** Tax-efficient and flexible — operated more like a holding company than a retail CEF.
- **No incentive fees.** Lowest-cost Pershing Square vehicle; structurally different from the offshore performance-fee funds.
- **Permanent capital compounding.** No redemptions, no rolling vintages — long-duration NAV growth is the entire point.
- **Insurance float model.** Vantage Holdings deploys insurance float into equities, à la Buffett.
- **Track record.** 19% ROE over 22 years; 24.9% over the 8 years of permanent capital.

### Structural and Strategic Innovation

**Why a closed-end fund?** PSUS adopts a closed-end fund legal structure not because it's a traditional fund, but because it is highly tax-efficient and flexible for their specific investment strategy. Ackman intends to operate it more like an investment holding company than a typical retail closed-end fund. Closed-end funds trade at a discount to NAV — the goal is to compress that discount over time through performance and communication.

**No incentive fees.** PSUS charges no incentive fees, making it the lowest-cost version of Pershing Square's offerings across all their vehicles. That's structurally different from the offshore funds which have historically charged performance fees.

**Permanent capital compounding.** Unlike traditional alternative asset managers that rely on constant fundraising — and shrink when they return capital — Ackman intends to grow PSUS primarily through long-term compounding of net asset value (NAV). The capital is permanent: no redemptions, no rolling vintage problem.

### Investment Philosophy and Market Outlook

**Current positioning.** Bullish: some of the highest-quality businesses globally are trading at historically low multiples. The firm focuses on concentrated, large-cap, liquid assets — a deliberately narrow mandate.

**The Berkshire insurance float model.** Ackman draws a direct parallel to Berkshire Hathaway's structure. Vantage Holdings (an insurer) will have its assets managed in a manner similar to how Buffett uses insurance float to compound value — patient, long-duration capital deployed into equities at attractive prices.

**SEC transparency advantage.** The listed, SEC-registered status of PSUS allows for more transparent investor communication than the restrictions placed on their previous offshore, private funds. Ackman has consistently argued that transparency is itself a competitive advantage in the long-duration compounding game.

### Track Record

- **19% return on equity** over the last 22 years
- **24.9% return** over the 8 years since adopting permanent capital

## All-In — OpenAI Misses, Machine-vs-Machine Cybersecurity, Capex, Vibecoding, Peptides

**[Watch on YouTube ↗](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fpC4sbawSzQ)** · Chamath, Sacks, Friedberg, Calacanis

> **TL;DR.** OpenAI's miss is supply-capped, not demand-capped. Cybersecurity flips to machine-vs-machine. Hyperscaler capex isn't 2000-Cisco — there are no dark GPUs. Retatrutide is the real triple-agonist deal.

- **OpenAI misses.** Below 1B WAU + revenue targets — but compute and power are the bottleneck, not user demand.
- **Cybersecurity.** GPT 5.5 + Anthropic "Mythos" enable white-hat hardening — a one-time upgrade cycle, then steady-state machine-vs-machine.
- **Musk vs. Altman.** Suit alleges breach of charitable trust in OpenAI's for-profit pivot — original-intent fight.
- **Capex.** No dark GPUs, no Cisco-2000 overhang — every chip purchased is utilized on arrival.
- **Vibecoding.** AI agent deleted a prod codebase during a credential fix — accountability + validation gates are non-negotiable.
- **Retatrutide.** Triple agonist (GLP-1/GIP/glucagon): real liver-fat reduction and notable muscle preservation vs GLP-1 monotherapy.

### OpenAI Misses Internal Targets — But Not Because of Demand

Reports surfaced this week that OpenAI fell short of internal goals for both user growth (the 1B weekly-active-user target) and revenue. The hosts argue the miss is not a demand problem — it's a **compute and power** bottleneck. Every hyperscaler is supply-capped on power for data centers, which mechanically benefits Amazon, Google, and Meta because they own the long-dated power and land. Developer interest is rotating toward OpenAI's new **"Spud" base model** and **GPT 5.5**, while Anthropic's **Opus 4.7** is taking heat for compute rationing and performance bugs.

### AI Cybersecurity (20:02–31:02) — One-Time Upgrade Cycle

## Money Stuff — PSUS Dual IPO, Multi-Strat Brutality, BDC Psychology, Avis Section-16 Dodge

**[Listen ↗](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=41wsYdkShKQ)** · Matt Levine + Katie Greifeld

> **TL;DR.** Four stories, one theme: elegant financial structures running into the physics of microstructure, talent economics, investor psychology, and securities-law tripwires.

- **PSUS dual IPO.** Closed-end fund + management-co IPO in one week; opened ~20% below NAV despite the 1/5 (and 3/10 anchor) sweetener.
- **Multi-strat math.** Bobby Jain returning to Millennium — pod economics only close at scale; LPs want steady 10–15% _now_, PMs want $30–100M+ comp _now_.
- **Boaz Weinstein BDC tender.** ~65¢-on-the-dollar tender on a non-traded BDC drew <1% — investors refuse to crystallize the loss they bought specifically to avoid seeing.
- **Avis squeeze.** SRS + Pentwater owned >100% of float; Pentwater sold ~$1.75B for ~$1B profit, settled Section-16 on ~94k shares (~$50M) and kept the rest by booking sells out of separately-held funds.

A classic episode: small talk on Hoboken mold and radon, then four meaty stories. Levine's running theme — financial structures that look elegant on paper run into the **physics** of market microstructure, talent economics, investor psychology, and securities-law tripwires.

### 1. Bill Ackman's Dual Pershing Square / PSUS IPO (~1:40)

Ackman took **two** things public in one week: **PSUS**, a new $5B closed-end fund holding large-caps, hedges, and Uber; and the **Pershing Square management company** itself. IPO buyers of PSUS automatically received **1/5 of a management-company share**; anchor investors got a sweeter **3/10**. The free management-co stock was the sweetener that got the closed-end fund subscribed.

**The physics of CEFs.** Closed-end funds issue a fixed number of shares and trade on-exchange. You can't redeem at NAV. As a structural matter, CEFs **almost always trade at a discount** — investors demand it because they're stuck with whatever price the market gives them. Ackman spent years promising PSUS would defy this. It opened at a **~20% discount**, drifted to the low-$40s, and the management company traded around $29–$30. IPO buyers slightly underwater; anchors slightly up; Ackman bought in the open market the next day.

**Why bother?** **Permanent capital.** Ackman wants to make long-duration, illiquid bets without the redemption risk that arbitrageurs exploit. He's framing PSUS as a Berkshire-style permanent vehicle, with Howard Hughes Corp. as an acquisition platform alongside it.

### 2. Multi-Strat Hedge Funds and Bobby Jain's Return to Millennium (12:45)

Bobby Jain — former Millennium co-president, the architect of much of its multi-strat machine — launched **Jain Global** in 2024 with ~$6B and is now returning outside capital and going home to manage money for Millennium only.

**Why new multi-strats almost never scale.** Multi-strat funds run dozens of independent pods, each a mini-business with high fixed costs (data, prime brokerage, salaries). PMs get paid **$30–100M+** on their own books via pass-through fees. Pods that are too small lose money on PM comp; pods that are too big run out of strategy capacity. LPs (pensions, endowments) want **steady 10–15% with low correlation, now** — they will not wait 5–10 years for scale. The economics only close at enormous AUM. Even Jain couldn't crack it in two years.

**Levine's analogy.** Tech inverts this — low cash, big equity, patient capital. Multi-strats demand cash now on both sides of the table. The math doesn't work until you're already huge.

### 3. Boaz Weinstein's Tender for OBDC2 (17:45)

Saba's Boaz Weinstein offered to buy 5–10% of Blue Owl's non-traded BDC at **~65¢ on the dollar**. Fill rate: **<1%**.

**BDC mechanics.** Non-traded BDCs lend to private middle-market borrowers, sell through advisors to retail/HNW, and offer quarterly redemptions at NAV (capped, e.g., 5% of fund per quarter). Investors bought the product **because** it doesn't visibly mark down. Even with secondary pricing implying a 35% discount, they refuse to crystallize the loss. They'd rather queue at 100¢ for years (or ignore the tender entirely — Levine: "I throw them away").

**Conclusion.** The tender was partly trolling, but it surfaced a useful read: private credit holders are not panicking, they are stubborn. Boaz may try the same play on longer-duration BDCs where redemption queues are longer and stale-NAV pressure is higher.

### 4. The Avis Short Squeeze and the Section 16 Workaround (22:31)

Avis ran from ~$100 to over **$700** and back to the $100s. Two funds — long-term holder **SRS** (board seat, locked up) and opportunistic **Pentwater** (~50% including derivatives) — together owned **more than 100% of the float**. Pentwater sold into the squeeze for **~$1.75B** with profits estimated **~$1B**.

**The Section 16 problem.** Owning >10% makes you an insider; buying and selling within six months triggers **short-swing profit disgorgement** — all profits go back to the company. Pentwater had doubled its stake earlier in the year, so on its face, the squeeze sale should have triggered the rule.

**The workaround.** Pentwater told Avis: **~94,000 shares** were short-swing — here's a check (~$50M or less). The **millions of other shares** were sold out of **different funds and accounts** that had held longer than six months or were structured separately, so no Section-16 liability. They kept the vast majority of the windfall.

**Aftermath.** Avis's earnings call was awkward; executives said they'd "look into anything we can do to recover for shareholders". Levine's read: the lawyers will fight over whether the separate-account structure really insulates the trade. Either way — caused/rode the squeeze, cashed at the top, dodged the trap. Epic trade.

### Levine's running thesis

Finance is full of structures that look great on paper but run into the physics: CEF discounts, multi-strat fixed costs, BDC investor psychology, Section-16 tripwires. The four stories of the week are four different versions of the same lesson.
