### All In podcast

- URL: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fpC4sbawSzQ

Description: The hosts discuss reports that OpenAI missed internal targets
for user growth (specifically the 1 billion weekly active user goal) and
revenue. They argue that these misses are not due to a lack of demand, but
rather compute and power constraints. The speakers emphasize that the entire
AI market is currently limited by the supply of power required to fuel data
centers, a bottleneck that benefits hyperscalers like Amazon, Google, and
Meta. They also note that developer interest is shifting toward OpenAI's new
'Spud' base model and GPT 5.5, while Anthropic's 'Opus 4.7' has faced
criticism for compute rationing and performance bugs.

**AI Cybersecurity (20:02 – 31:02).** The conversation explores the dual
nature of AI in cybersecurity. While models like GPT 5.5 and Anthropic's
'Mythos' demonstrate advanced capabilities in simulating cyberattacks, the
hosts view this as a necessary one-time upgrade cycle. By using these models
for 'white hat' defense and vulnerability discovery, the industry can harden
existing, inherently insecure human-written code. The hosts predict that in
the coming years, most software will be rewritten by machines, transitioning
the field toward a 'machines vs. machines' security paradigm.

**Elon Musk vs. Sam Altman Lawsuit (31:03 – 40:59).** The podcast touches on
the ongoing trial where Elon Musk alleges that OpenAI committed a breach of
charitable trust and unjust enrichment by pivoting from a non-profit to a
for-profit structure. The hosts highlight Musk's demand for significant
damages and the reversal of OpenAI's corporate structure, noting the legal
tension regarding the original intentions of the organization.

**Big Tech Earnings and Capex Explosion (41:00 – 52:43).** The hosts analyze
the massive capital expenditures (Capex) from hyperscalers like Google,
Microsoft, and Amazon. Despite a reduction in free cash flow, they argue
this is a structural shift, not a bubble akin to the 2000s Cisco era. They
differentiate current spending by noting that, unlike the 'dark fiber' of
the past, there is no such thing as 'dark GPUs' today — all purchased
compute is being utilized immediately due to voracious demand.

**Vibecoding Risks (52:44 – 58:32).** Reflecting on a recent incident where
an AI agent deleted an entire codebase during a routine credential fix, the
hosts warn against 'vibecoding' — the practice of relying on AI for complex
system administration without rigorous supervision. They stress that AI
often lacks an understanding of when to stop or request confirmation,
making human accountability and validation essential for professional
software development.

**Peptide Trends (58:33 – 1:06:33).** The hosts discuss the mainstream
emergence of Retatrutide, a triple-agonist peptide that targets GLP1, GIP,
and glucagon receptors. They note the clinical data showing significant
weight loss, liver fat reduction, and A1C improvements. They also explore
the cultural trend of 'peptides' as a tool for health optimization, while
cautioning that these are significant interventions requiring proper
planning.

**Friedberg's Supreme Court Experience (1:06:34 – 1:20:57).** David
Friedberg shares his experience attending oral arguments at the Supreme
Court regarding a case involving Monsanto/Roundup. He describes the
procedural rigor, where the Court focuses on the legal interpretation of
federal statutes versus state authority, rather than re-litigating facts.
He highlights the intensity of the questioning by the justices and the
challenge of distilling complex regulatory issues into a 60-minute
argument.

### Money Stuff (Matt Levine + Katie Greifeld)

- URL: https://youtu.be/41wsYdkShKQ?si=K4UmEGbaS2kFLAtK
- Tags: Close down fund at discount to NAV, Boaz Weinstein offer to OBDC,
  Avis short squeeze.

**Episode Overview.** Classic Money Stuff episode with Matt Levine
(Bloomberg columnist and host) and Katie Greifeld (Bloomberg News
reporter/TV anchor). They chat casually at the start about real-life
annoyances (homeownership, mold remediation in Hoboken, radon checks), then
dive into four meaty financial stories from the week. The tone is
conversational, witty, deeply analytical — Matt loves the "physics" of
market structures, Katie keeps pushing on practical "why would anyone do
this?" questions.

**Four main topics:**

1. Bill Ackman's dual Pershing Square USA (PSUS) IPO
2. Why launching a new multi-strategy hedge fund is brutally hard (Bobby
   Jain case study)
3. Investor psychology in private-credit non-traded BDCs (Boaz Weinstein's
   tender offer)
4. The wild Avis short squeeze and the clever Section 16 workaround by
   Pentwater Capital

#### 1. Pershing Square USA (PSUS) Closed-End Fund IPO (~1:40)

**What happened.** Bill Ackman finally took two things public in one week:

- **PSUS** — a new closed-end fund that raised **$5 billion** to invest in
  large-cap stocks, credit hedges, Uber, etc.
- **Pershing Square** — the actual hedge-fund management company itself.

It was a "dual IPO." Investors who bought PSUS shares in the IPO also
automatically received **1/5 of a share** of the management company (anchor
investors got a sweeter 3/10). The idea was that the free management-
company equity would sweeten the deal enough to get people to buy the
closed-end fund.

**The "physics" of closed-end funds (Matt's favorite framing).** Closed-end
funds (CEFs) issue a fixed number of shares and trade on the stock exchange
like a normal company. Unlike open-end mutual funds or ETFs, you can't
redeem shares directly from the fund at net asset value (NAV). → Result:
CEFs almost always trade at a discount to NAV (often 10–20%+). Investors
demand a discount because they're stuck with whatever price the market
gives them, and the manager can't be fired easily.

Ackman spent years promising this one would be different — would trade at
a premium to NAV, like a "Berkshire Hathaway" with permanent capital (no
redemptions). Even wrote a letter saying it required a "significant leap of
faith."

Reality: PSUS launched, traded at ~20% discount to NAV on day one,
recovered a bit to ~$43.50 (still well below NAV), and the management
company traded around $29–$30. IPO buyers slightly underwater; anchor
investors slightly up.

**Why the structure?** Permanent capital — long-term, illiquid bets without
redemption risk. Compared it to Berkshire and Howard Hughes Corp. Katie and
Matt are skeptical: "How do these two vehicles not compete with each
other?"

**Hosts' take.** Matt: "It just seems extremely hard to defy the physics of
closed-end funds." Katie: The grandiosity ("$25 billion at a premium!")
fizzled into a normal $5 billion CEF trading at a discount. Still, Ackman
bought shares in the open market the next day to show confidence.

#### 2. Multi-Strategy Hedge Funds — Bobby Jain Returns to Millennium (12:45)

**What happened.** Bobby Jain, former Millennium co-president and architect
of its multi-strat platform, launched his own fund (Jain Global) in 2024
with ~$6B. Two years later he's returning outside capital and going back to
managing money only for Millennium. "He's going home."

**Why new multi-strats almost never scale.** Multi-strat funds run dozens
of independent "pods" or desks. Each pod has huge fixed costs (salaries,
tech, data, prime brokerage). PMs get paid **$30–100M+ a year** based on
their book ("pass-through" fees). Too little capital → still pay PM, lose
money. Too much capital → returns drop. LPs (pensions, endowments) want
steady 10–15% with low correlation **now**. They will not wait 5–10 years.

**Hosts' analogy.** Matt: opposite of a tech startup. Tech employees take
low cash + huge equity, investors wait for the payday. Multi-strats: PMs
want $50M cash now, LPs want steady returns now. Math doesn't work until
enormous.

**Key takeaway.** Even Bobby Jain — who helped build Millennium's machine
— couldn't crack it in two years.

#### 3. Boaz Weinstein BDC Tender Offer (17:45)

**What happened.** Boaz Weinstein (Saba Capital) made a public tender offer
for up to 5–10% of Blue Owl's non-traded BDC (OBDC2) at a big discount
(~65¢ on the dollar). He got **less than 1%** filled.

**BDC basics.** BDCs lend to private middle-market companies. "Non-traded"
ones sold to retail/wealthy via advisors; promise steady yields and
quarterly redemptions at NAV (100¢) but with caps (e.g., 5% of fund per
quarter).

**Investor psychology.** These investors bought the product because it's
supposed to be stable. Even when secondary pricing implies a 35% discount,
most refuse to sell to Boaz at 65¢. Matt (who owns a slice of a different
Blue Owl BDC): "I get these tender offers in the mail and throw them
away."

**Hosts' conclusion.** Tender mostly trolling, but revealed that
private-credit investors aren't panicking — they're stubborn. Boaz may try
again on longer-duration BDCs.

#### 4. Avis Short Squeeze + Section 16 Workaround (22:31)

**What happened.** Avis went from ~$100 to over **$700**, then collapsed
back to the $100s. Two hedge funds together owned more than 100% of the
actual float:

- **SRS** — long-term holder with board seat, locked up.
- **Pentwater** — opportunistic; ramped up to ~50% (including derivatives).

When the squeeze hit its peak, Pentwater sold hundreds of millions of
dollars of stock (proceeds ~$1.75B, profits ~$1B).

**The Section 16 problem.** Anyone owning >10% is an "insider." Buy + sell
within six months → must disgorge all profits back to the company
(short-swing rule). Pentwater had doubled its stake in Feb/Mar.

**The workaround.** Pentwater told Avis:

- On ~94,000 shares: yes, Section 16. Here's a check (~$50M or less).
- On the millions of other shares: sold from different funds/accounts that
  had owned the stock for longer than six months (or were structured
  separately) — no short-swing liability.

Result: Pentwater kept the vast majority; Avis got a tiny "tip."

**Aftermath.** Avis' earnings call awkward. Executives "looking into
anything we can do to recover for shareholders." Matt and Katie suspect
lawyers will fight over whether the separate-account structure really
works.

**Hosts' reaction.** Matt: "I thought they literally couldn't sell because
of Section 16… I was naïve." Both: "Something feels weird about how they
bought so much in one place and sold in another, but… great trade."

#### Final Notes

Episode ends with usual sign-off and plugs. Overall: finance is full of
structures that look great on paper but run into real-world physics
(closed-end discounts, talent costs, investor psychology, securities-law
tripwires).

**My notes:** Avis short squeeze where they returned 94k shares of profit
but the billion they kept; 2 funds (Pentwater + SRS) have most equity;
Section 16 of the SEC code; PSUS gave 1/5 shares of management company and
3/10 for anchors of management company.

### Uncapped (Jack Altman) — Tarek Mansour / Kalshi

- URL: https://youtu.be/Tt6ozrcjLyQ?si=tQPiQJnTydmpYYwt

This conversation follows the journey of Tarek Mansour, co-founder and CEO
of Kalshi, a regulated prediction market exchange.

**Tarek's Background and Genesis.**

- Born in California, raised in Lebanon. Developed an early interest in
  math amidst a volatile environment (0:29 – 0:42).
- Attended MIT, later worked in finance at Goldman Sachs and Citadel,
  where he noticed that many trading ideas stemmed from simple opinions on
  future events rather than traditional asset analysis (0:59 – 1:21).
- The idea for Kalshi emerged from the observation that traditional
  markets were inefficient for expressing views on specific outcomes (e.g.
  Brexit or the 2016 Trump election), which led to "backwards" trading
  strategies (1:23 – 3:55).

**Hackathon and Early Hurdles.**

- October 2018, YC hackathon. Despite judges initially telling them their
  idea was not allowed in the U.S., they won (6:42 – 7:15).
- Y Combinator was difficult — no product, no customers, fully focused on
  regulatory approval (7:25 – 8:02).

**Regulatory Battles and Legal Milestones.**

- Years navigating regulators, eventually receiving approval for a
  regulated exchange in November 2020 (11:06 – 11:38).
- After multiple delays and the blocking of their election market in
  2023, sued the government (15:00 – 15:35).
- Won the landmark case in October 2024, redefining the distinction
  between gambling and financial markets and establishing the platform as
  a regulated exchange and clearing house (17:21 – 18:32).

**Technical and Market Structure.**

- Not a casino: open free market where users trade against each other,
  Kalshi takes a small transaction fee (18:36 – 18:50).
- Hedging use cases: inflation prints, Fed decisions, weather events
  (35:51 – 38:00).
- ~127 people, no traditional managerial layers; high-output autonomy and
  self-organizing structure (41:47 – 43:30).

**My notes:** Tarek's story; hedging on real life because it's more fair;
options etc. you cannot beat hedge funds; hackathon where they built an
order book; firing is hard; 2024 landmark case, not gambling.

### Stripe Sessions 2026 — Keynote

- URL: https://www.youtube.com/live/e13-s0p1tfE?si=1kG4LNFsC9bbZEm7

The Stripe Sessions 2026 keynote introduces Stripe's strategy to build the
economic infrastructure for the AI era, focusing on tools for an economy
driven by autonomous AI agents (2:50 – 8:36).

**Key Product Launches and Technical Capabilities.**

- **Stripe Projects (11:02 – 14:17).** Programmatic deployment of
  applications, allowing developers to manage services, databases (e.g.,
  Supabase), and hosting (e.g., Vercel) through AI agents.
- **Agentic Commerce Suite (19:40 – 22:27).** Designed to make products
  legible to agents, enabling autonomous buying and selling. Includes
  support for the Model Payment Protocol (MPP) for agent-to-agent
  transactions.
- **Checkout Studio (24:01 – 26:31).** Visual builder + dashboard for
  configuring, analyzing, and optimizing high-performing checkout flows,
  including custom embedded forms.
- **Stripe Database (57:48 – 58:10).** Postgres-based offering allowing
  developers and their agents to access and query Stripe business data in
  real time.
- **Metronome and Tempo (45:47 – 50:55).** Real-time metering, rating,
  and payment streaming — new business models where payments occur as
  tokens are consumed.
- **Stripe Signals (1:27:28 – 1:28:09).** Intelligence layer providing
  real-time risk scores and insights on customers / transactions, via API.
- **Stripe Treasury (1:40:15 – 1:48:21).** Financial OS — multicurrency,
  FDIC-insured US balances, payouts to 160 countries, stablecoin rails.

**Strategic Updates.**

- **Platform Growth Studio (1:54:31 – 1:56:51).** Recommendation engine
  surfacing growth opportunities across platforms.
- **Public Roadmap (2:08:33 – 2:09:12).** Comprehensive roadmap at
  stripe.com/roadmap through Q1 2027.
- **Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP) (2:03:01 – 2:04:10).** Stripe joined
  the general committee of an industry-wide initiative to standardize the
  commerce journey across platforms.

**Two protocols.**

**Model Payment Protocol — MPP (14:59 – 18:20).**

- Open standard enabling agents to purchase goods and services
  autonomously. Solves the non-trivial challenge of agents interacting
  with pricing pages, forms, and human-designed payment flows.
- Functions as a communication layer that allows a service to notify an
  agent that a payment is required, enabling direct value exchange between
  two autonomous agents. Integrates with the Link wallet, allowing
  agent-initiated payments that a human user can review and authorize via
  biometrics.

**Universal Commerce Protocol — UCP (2:03:01 – 2:04:16).**

- Eliminates fragmentation across the commerce journey by providing a
  single protocol that supports any platform. A unified standard for making
  transactions trustworthy across the entire internet commerce lifecycle.
- Pioneered by Stripe and Google. Recently joined by Meta, Amazon,
  Salesforce, and Microsoft on the UCP general committee. Goal: one
  standard to build on, one specification to implement, one community to
  support — accelerating agentic commerce.
